# StoryBrand Messaging - Implementation Guide

Step-by-step methodology for building clear brand messaging using Donald Miller's 7-part narrative framework, where the customer is the hero.

## The SB7 Framework Overview

StoryBrand is built on the structure of classic storytelling. Every successful story has the same pattern — and your marketing should follow it:

```
A CHARACTER (customer as hero)
has a PROBLEM (internal + external + philosophical)
meets a GUIDE (your brand — with empathy and authority)
who gives them a PLAN (your process, made clear)
and CALLS THEM TO ACTION (direct CTA + transitional CTA)
that helps them AVOID FAILURE (stakes: what's lost without you)
and ends in SUCCESS (the transformation you deliver)
```

## Step 1: Define the Character (Your Customer as Hero)

The customer is the hero, not your brand. Your brand is Yoda; the customer is Luke.

**1a. Identify ONE primary desire**
- What does your customer want most in relation to your product?
- State it simply: not "they want marketing software" but "they want more customers without wasting time on marketing that doesn't work"
- Resist listing multiple desires. One clear desire is far more powerful.

**1b. The desire must be:**
- Specific (not "grow their business" but "triple their demo bookings in 90 days")
- Attainable (a customer can imagine having it)
- Connected to your actual product capability

## Step 2: Define the Problem (Three Levels)

Great stories have villains. Identify the villain (external problem), how it makes the hero feel (internal problem), and why it shouldn't be this way (philosophical problem).

**2a. External problem**
- The surface-level, practical problem your product solves
- Example: "Their landing page isn't converting visitors into leads"

**2b. Internal problem**
- The emotional frustration beneath the external problem
- Example: "They feel like they're wasting money on ads for nothing; they doubt whether they can grow this business"
- This is what the customer actually buys a solution to

**2c. Philosophical problem**
- The "it just shouldn't be this way" dimension
- Example: "Good businesses that solve real problems deserve to reach customers who need them"
- This is what makes your brand feel like it stands for something

**2d. The villain framing**
- Name the villain (the problem, not a person)
- The villain makes your company relevant: if there were no villain, there would be no story
- Examples: wasted time, hidden complexity, opaque pricing, disconnected teams

## Step 3: Position Your Brand as the Guide

The guide enters the story after the hero has a problem. The guide never solves the problem for the hero — the guide equips the hero to solve it.

**3a. Demonstrate empathy first**
- "We know how frustrating it is when [their problem]. You shouldn't have to [the unfair part]."
- Empathy opens the relationship. Authority builds trust.

**3b. Establish authority (without bragging)**
- Proof of results: "Over 2,400 teams have used this to [measurable outcome]"
- Credibility through specificity: named clients, press logos, specific metrics
- Endorsed by recognized authorities, not self-proclaimed expertise

**3c. Avoid the hero/guide confusion**
- Do not open with your company's story, awards, or history
- Every sentence should pass the test: "Is this about us, or about our customer?"
- Start with the customer's desire or problem — not your founding story

## Step 4: Give Them a Plan

Confusion is the enemy of conversion. Customers need to know exactly what to do and what will happen.

**4a. The 3-step process plan**
- Reduce all complexity to three clear steps
- Name each step with an action verb
- Example: "1. Book a call → 2. We build your plan → 3. Watch your pipeline grow"
- Three steps feel achievable; more feels overwhelming

**4b. The agreement plan (advanced)**
- For high-trust or high-ticket products, an "agreement plan" reduces anxiety by naming and defusing fears
- Example: "We'll never lock you in with a contract. We'll give you full data export at any time. We'll be transparent about pricing before every billing cycle."
- This removes the friction of imagined worst-case scenarios

**4c. Make the plan visual**
- Put the 3-step plan in a visual diagram on the website
- "How it works" sections are consistently among the highest-converting sections on B2B homepages

## Step 5: Call Them to Action

Customers don't act without a clear, repeated call to action. Most websites are too timid.

**5a. Direct CTA**
- A specific, bold action: "Book a demo," "Start free trial," "Get your quote"
- This CTA should appear in: the header, the middle of the page, and above the footer
- No vague CTAs: "Learn more" or "Explore" are not calls to action

**5b. Transitional CTA (for not-yet-ready visitors)**
- Captures leads who aren't ready to buy but are interested
- A lower-stakes offer: free guide, assessment, case study, newsletter
- Position this alongside — not instead of — the direct CTA

**5c. CTA language principles**
- Use the customer's language, not your internal language
- "Start saving time" not "Activate your account"
- Lead with the outcome: "Get more leads" not "Sign up"

## Step 6: Define What's at Stake (Failure and Success)

Stories have stakes. If there are no consequences, there is no reason to act.

**6a. Acknowledge the cost of inaction**
- State what happens if they don't solve the problem
- Be specific: "Without a clear sales process, your best reps will close differently, your pipeline will be unpredictable, and your board will ask uncomfortable questions every quarter"
- This is not fear-mongering — it's honest acknowledgment of real consequences

**6b. Paint the success state vividly**
- The transformation after your product: what does their life or work look like?
- Use "after" language: "Imagine starting every Monday knowing exactly which deals will close this month"
- Connect to both external (business metrics) and internal (emotional) success

**6c. The resolution**
- Tie the success state back to the original desire stated in Step 1
- The customer's journey from desire → problem → guide → plan → action → success should feel complete

## Step 7: Build the Brand Script

Compile the SB7 elements into a single Brand Script document that all messaging draws from.

```
HERO:       Our customer is [character] who wants [primary desire].

PROBLEM:    They face [external problem], which makes them feel [internal frustration].
            And it's just wrong that [philosophical dimension].

GUIDE:      We understand [empathy statement]. That's why [authority statement with proof].

PLAN:       Here's how it works: 1) [Step 1] 2) [Step 2] 3) [Step 3]

CTA:        Direct: [Call to action button copy]
            Transitional: [Low-stakes offer]

FAILURE:    Without this, [specific negative consequence].

SUCCESS:    With this, [vivid transformation: external + internal outcome].
```

## Step 8: Apply the Brand Script to Every Asset

The Brand Script is the source of truth. Use it to write:
- Website homepage (H1 = primary desire; hero section = character + problem + CTA)
- Email subject lines (lead with internal problem or success state)
- Ad copy (external problem + CTA; or success state + CTA)
- Sales deck (guide → plan → success → avoid failure)
- Product one-pagers (3-step plan + direct CTA)
- LinkedIn bios (guide positioning: empathy + authority + CTA)

## Common Pitfalls

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Brand as hero | Customers tune out; they don't care about your story | Start with the customer's desire, not your founding |
| Multiple desires | Message is muddled | Pick one primary desire; everything else is secondary |
| Vague success state | Not motivating | Describe success with specific, sensory details |
| Missing stakes | No urgency | Explicitly name the cost of inaction |
| 5-step plan | Feels overwhelming | Reduce to three steps maximum |

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Primary customer desire identified (one sentence, specific)
- [ ] External, internal, and philosophical problems defined
- [ ] Brand empathy statement written (speaks to internal problem)
- [ ] Brand authority evidence gathered (specific, verifiable)
- [ ] 3-step plan written with action-verb step names
- [ ] Direct CTA copy written (outcome language)
- [ ] Transitional CTA offer identified
- [ ] Failure state articulated (specific consequences)
- [ ] Success state described (external + internal transformation)
- [ ] Brand Script document completed and shared with team
