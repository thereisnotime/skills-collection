# Influence Psychology - Implementation Guide

Step-by-step methodology for applying Cialdini's six principles of ethical persuasion to product design, copy, and sales.

## Step 1: Audit Current Persuasion Assets

Score each principle in your current product or page (0-3 per principle):

| Principle | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----------|---|---|---|---|
| Reciprocity | No free value given | Generic freebie | Valuable, unexpected gift | Personalized, high-value upfront offer |
| Commitment | No consistency triggers | Generic progress indicator | Small commitment requested | Progressive commitment ladder |
| Social Proof | No proof | Vague claims | Specific numbers/logos | Dynamic, recent, relevant testimonials |
| Authority | No credentials | Generic "experts" | Named credentials, press | Third-party endorsement from recognized authority |
| Liking | No relationship | Brand personality | Similarity signals | Personal connection established |
| Scarcity | No urgency | Generic "limited" | Legitimate scarcity stated | Specific, credible, time-bound scarcity |

Total score < 10 = significant persuasion gap. Fix the three lowest-scoring principles first.

## Step 2: Apply Reciprocity

Principle: Give first; people feel a deep obligation to return favors.

**2a. Identify the right gift**
- It must be: genuinely valuable, personalized (or feel like it is), and unexpected
- Examples: a free audit of the prospect's specific situation, a custom checklist, an industry report with their segment highlighted
- Wrong: generic ebooks, discount codes, free trials with no setup help

**2b. The reciprocity sequence**
1. Give something valuable before asking for anything
2. Make sure the prospect knows the value of what they're receiving
3. Ask only after demonstrating good faith
4. Keep the gift relevant to what you're eventually asking them to do

**2c. Product implementation**
- Onboarding: show the product's value before requiring payment or full setup
- Sales: send a personalized "pre-meeting research brief" before a call
- Marketing: publish genuinely useful free tools or frameworks (Hubspot's free CRM, Stripe's payment docs)

## Step 3: Apply Commitment and Consistency

Principle: Once people commit to something, they align their behavior with that commitment to avoid cognitive dissonance.

**3a. Design micro-commitments**
- Ask for small, public, voluntary commitments before asking for large ones
- Example sequence for SaaS signup:
  1. "What's your biggest challenge?" (identifies with the problem)
  2. "What's your goal for this month?" (commits to an outcome)
  3. "Let's set up your account to work toward that goal" (now setup feels like follow-through)

**3b. Written commitments are stronger**
- Asking users to type or check their goals creates a stronger commitment than simply reading them
- Onboarding wizards that ask users to "set up" their preferences activate consistency more than passive reading

**3c. Public commitments are strongest**
- "Share your goal with your team" links during onboarding
- Public accountability features (Beachbody "I committed to 90 days" posts)
- Community challenges where completion is visible

## Step 4: Apply Social Proof

Principle: In uncertainty, people look to the behavior of others to decide what to do.

**4a. Match proof to prospect type**

| Prospect Type | Most Effective Social Proof |
|--------------|-----------------------------|
| Similar industry | "500 [their industry] companies use this" |
| Skeptical buyer | Independent review site ratings (G2, Capterra) |
| Risk-averse | Named enterprise customers with logos |
| Individual | Peer testimonials ("As a [their role]...") |

**4b. Specificity rules**
- Bad: "Customers love us"
- Good: "4.8/5 from 2,400 verified reviews on G2"
- Better: "Sarah K., Head of Engineering at [Company]: 'Reduced our deploy time from 3 days to 4 hours'"

**4c. Placement for maximum impact**
- At the point of first impression (hero section): volume + authority logos
- At point of hesitation (pricing page): risk-reducing testimonials from buyers like the prospect
- Near the CTA: most recent testimonial (recency = credibility)
- In the product: usage stats, community activity indicators

## Step 5: Apply Authority

Principle: People defer to credible, legitimate expertise.

**5a. Types of authority signals**

| Signal | Implementation |
|--------|---------------|
| Credentials | "Founded by [Title] at [Credible Company]" or specific certifications |
| Press coverage | Media logos (use the "As seen in" section) |
| Publishing authority | Book, academic research, keynotes at recognized conferences |
| Endorsement | Named endorsement from a recognized authority in the space |
| Track record | Specific, verifiable results at scale ("$2B in transactions processed") |

**5b. Third-party authority beats self-claimed authority**
- Never just say "we're experts"
- Quote a third party saying you're an expert (analyst report, customer quote, press article)

**5c. Authority in copy**
- Use specific numbers rather than general claims: "helped 2,400 companies" not "helped thousands"
- Cite sources for data claims
- Name credentials that are meaningful to the specific audience (CISSP for security buyers, not for e-commerce buyers)

## Step 6: Apply Liking

Principle: People buy from people and brands they like and feel similar to.

**6a. Build similarity**
- Mirror the language, values, and worldview of your target audience
- Show people who look like the target customer in photos, testimonials, and case studies
- Use "we" language that implies shared identity: "We believe engineers deserve better tools"

**6b. Reveal genuine behind-the-scenes content**
- Transparency and imperfection build liking (the "pratfall effect" — likable people seem more human after small mistakes)
- Behind-the-scenes product development, founder stories, honest company updates

**6c. Reduce friction through familiarity**
- Consistent brand voice across all touchpoints reduces cognitive friction
- Use the same terminology as your audience uses for their problems — not your internal vocabulary

## Step 7: Apply Scarcity

Principle: Things that are rare or becoming unavailable are perceived as more valuable.

**7a. Only use legitimate scarcity**
- False scarcity ("Only 3 left!" when there are hundreds) destroys trust when discovered
- Legitimate scarcity types:
  - Time-limited pricing (annual promotion, launch discount with end date)
  - Cohort-limited access (only 50 spots in this month's onboarding cohort due to support capacity)
  - Invitation-only access (beta list, referral-only)

**7b. Make scarcity specific and credible**
- Explain WHY the scarcity exists: "We limit cohorts to 50 to maintain a 4:1 student-to-coach ratio"
- Show the countdown or remaining count only when it's genuinely limited

**7c. Loss aversion framing**
- People are more motivated to avoid losing something than to gain an equivalent thing
- Frame offers in terms of what's lost by not acting: "After March 31, the price increases to $299/month"
- Show what's at stake in terms of the missed opportunity, not just the discount

## Common Pitfalls

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Fake scarcity | Trust destroyed when discovered | Only use real scarcity with explanation |
| Generic social proof | Low credibility | Specific, recent, role-relevant proof |
| Front-loading the ask | Triggers reactance before reciprocity established | Give first, ask second |
| All six principles deployed simultaneously | Feels manipulative | Use 2-3 principles per touchpoint, naturally |
| Authority claims without verification | Prospects Google and disprove | All authority claims must be independently verifiable |

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Persuasion audit scored per principle (0-3 each)
- [ ] Three weakest principles identified for immediate improvement
- [ ] Reciprocity gift identified: specific, valuable, relevant
- [ ] Commitment ladder mapped: 3 escalating micro-commitments
- [ ] Social proof matched to prospect type with specificity
- [ ] Authority signals verified as independently credible
- [ ] Scarcity claims reviewed: all legitimate with explanations
- [ ] Copy reviewed for liking signals: similarity, transparency, shared language
