# Obviously Awesome - Implementation Guide

Step-by-step methodology for building product positioning using April Dunford's five-component positioning framework.

## Step 1: Understand What Positioning Actually Is

Positioning is not a tagline, a mission statement, or a value proposition. It is the context that tells a customer how to evaluate your product and why it matters.

Positioning determines:
- What category a customer places you in
- What alternatives they compare you against
- Which of your features they pay attention to
- How they judge your value

Get positioning right, and everything downstream (messaging, pricing, sales) becomes easier. Get it wrong, and no amount of clever copy will fix it.

## Step 2: Assemble Your Positioning Team

Positioning is not a solo exercise. Get these perspectives in the room:

| Role | Why They're Essential |
|------|----------------------|
| Sales | Hears what customers actually compare you to |
| Customer Success | Knows why customers stay and churn |
| Product | Understands unique capabilities deeply |
| Marketing | Tests positioning in market response |
| Founder/CEO | Has access to original positioning assumptions |

Run a positioning workshop (half-day minimum). Separate information-gathering from decision-making.

## Step 3: List Your Competitive Alternatives

Positioning only makes sense relative to alternatives. Customers will always compare you to something.

**3a. Ask sales this question:**
"When we lose a deal, what does the customer choose instead?"

**3b. Possible answers include:**
- A direct competitor (another product doing the same thing)
- A category-adjacent product (solving the same problem differently)
- A DIY approach (spreadsheets, manual processes)
- Doing nothing (the problem isn't important enough to solve)
- Hiring someone (outsourcing the problem)

**3c. Document all real competitive alternatives**
- Do not include theoretical alternatives that never appear in real deals
- Do not include aspirational competitors ("we're really competing with enterprise X") if your customers don't say so
- Group alternatives by type: direct, indirect, non-consumption

## Step 4: Isolate Your Unique Attributes

Features are what your product does. Attributes are capabilities that matter to customers and that you have in ways competitors do not.

**4a. List every differentiating feature**
- Start comprehensive — list anything that is meaningfully different about your product
- Include: technical capabilities, integrations, support model, pricing structure, data, speed, depth

**4b. Filter for uniqueness**
- Cross-reference each feature against competitive alternatives
- Remove anything that competitors also have or can easily match
- What's left = genuine differentiators

**4c. Translate features to value**
- For each differentiating feature, ask: "So what? What does this mean for the customer's work or life?"
- Feature: "API-first architecture"
- Value: "Your team can embed this anywhere in your workflow without switching tools"

## Step 5: Map Attributes to Customer Value

Not all attributes are valuable to all customers. Map each differentiating attribute to the specific value it creates for specific customer types.

| Attribute | Value Created | Customer Who Cares Most |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| Real-time sync | No version conflicts when collaborating | Teams with 5+ collaborators |
| SOC 2 certified | Procurement approval is fast | Enterprise buyers with security reviews |
| No-code setup | Live in hours, not weeks | Small teams without dedicated engineering |

This mapping is the foundation of your positioning — and it reveals which customers you can serve best.

## Step 6: Identify Best-Fit Customers

Your product is not for everyone. Positioning requires choosing who you serve best.

**6a. Characteristics of best-fit customers**
- They get the most value from your unique attributes
- They are easy to sell to (short sales cycle, fewer objections)
- They renew at high rates and expand usage
- They refer others like themselves

**6b. Analyze your current customer base**
- Who are your most profitable, happiest, longest-retained customers?
- What do they have in common? (industry, role, company stage, workflow, problem type)
- Build a profile from real data, not aspirations

**6c. Define the "when" (trigger context)**
- Best-fit customers have a specific situation that makes your product the right answer
- "Customers in [situation], trying to accomplish [goal], who have [attribute] and lack [gap], are our best fit"

## Step 7: Choose or Create Your Market Category

The market category is the box you put yourself in. It sets the competitive comparison frame and the customer's buying expectations.

**7a. Three market category strategies:**

| Strategy | When to Use | Risk |
|----------|-------------|------|
| Existing category | Your differentiation is clear within it; category awareness is high | Compared directly to incumbents |
| New category | No existing category fits; you're creating new behavior | High education burden; "is this real?" skepticism |
| Repositioning an existing category | Category exists but is defined wrong for your advantage | Requires sufficient market influence |

**7b. Choosing the right category**
- Which category makes your unique attributes the most important evaluation criteria?
- Which category positions your biggest competitor as least relevant?
- Which category do your best-fit customers already know and trust?

**7c. Name your category with care**
- Category names should be descriptive and credible, not creative
- "Account-Based Marketing" works because buyers know what it means. "Revenue Orchestration Platform" does not.

## Step 8: Write the Positioning Statement

The positioning statement is an internal document, not a tagline. It guides all downstream messaging.

**Format:**
```
For [best-fit customer profile]
who need to [key job or goal],
[product name] is a [market category]
that [primary differentiating value],
unlike [competitive alternative]
which [the key gap that makes the alternative wrong for this customer].
```

**Example:**
```
For senior engineers at Series A-C startups
who need to review, merge, and ship code without slowing down their team,
Linear is a project management tool
that keeps engineering workflows as the source of truth with zero overhead,
unlike Jira
which creates administrative burden that engineers abandon within weeks.
```

## Common Pitfalls

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Positioning to everyone | No message resonates strongly with anyone | Pick your best-fit customer explicitly |
| Copying competitor positioning | Me-too positioning with no differentiation | Position against competitors, not with them |
| Leading with features, not value | Features aren't memorable without context | Always translate features to customer outcomes |
| Skipping competitive alternative analysis | Positioning built in a vacuum | Ground all positioning in real competitive comparisons |
| Treating positioning as a marketing exercise | Sales, product, and support all pull in different directions | Run positioning as a cross-functional workshop |

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Positioning team assembled (sales, CS, product, marketing, leadership)
- [ ] Competitive alternatives listed from real win/loss data (not assumptions)
- [ ] Differentiating features listed and filtered for genuine uniqueness
- [ ] Each feature translated to customer value
- [ ] Best-fit customer profile defined with real customer analysis
- [ ] Market category selected (existing, new, or repositioned)
- [ ] Positioning statement written in the 6-component format
- [ ] Positioning socialized with sales team for feedback
