# Crossing the Chasm - Implementation Guide

Step-by-step methodology for transitioning a technology product from early adopters to the mainstream market, based on Geoffrey Moore's framework.

## Step 1: Diagnose Your Position in the Adoption Lifecycle

Identify where your product currently sits:

| Stage | Customer Profile | Signs You're Here |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Innovators | Tech enthusiasts, love novelty | Customers use it despite rough UX |
| Early Adopters | Visionaries, seeking competitive edge | Customers sponsor development, tolerate bugs |
| **THE CHASM** | — | Sales stall after initial momentum |
| Early Majority | Pragmatists, want proven solutions | Customers ask "Who else uses this?" |
| Late Majority | Conservatives, skeptical of change | Customers want turnkey, no-risk options |
| Laggards | Skeptics, buy only when forced | Forced adoption via regulation or default |

**Chasm indicators:**
- Referrals stop coming from existing customers
- Sales cycles lengthen dramatically
- Prospects cite "no reference customers like us"
- Revenue growth plateaus after early success

## Step 2: Select Your Beachhead Segment (D-Day Strategy)

Do not try to cross the chasm to "the mainstream market." Pick one specific segment to win completely first.

**2a. Identify 3-5 candidate beachhead segments**
- Each must be a specific, nameable group with a shared problem
- Example: not "mid-market SaaS" but "Series B SaaS companies with a VP of Engineering and no dedicated DevOps team"

**2b. Score each segment on 6 criteria (0-3 per criterion)**

| Criterion | Question | Score |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Target customer | Is there a specific, reachable buyer? | /3 |
| Compelling reason | Is the problem forcing action now? | /3 |
| Whole product | Can you deliver a complete solution today? | /3 |
| Competition | Is the segment not already owned by an incumbent? | /3 |
| Partners/allies | Do you have the ecosystem to serve this segment? | /3 |
| Distribution | Do you have access to reach these buyers? | /3 |

Select the segment with the highest score. This is your beachhead. Commit fully.

**2c. The "bowling pin" expansion plan**
- Map adjacent segments that will naturally follow once you own the beachhead
- The beachhead segment must have maximum referral influence over adjacent segments
- Example: win DevOps engineers at Series B SaaS → adjacent: DevOps at Series C SaaS, then enterprise DevOps

## Step 3: Build the Whole Product

Early adopters buy vision. Pragmatists buy a whole product — a complete solution to their problem, not just your core technology.

**3a. Map the whole product gap**
- List every element required for the beachhead customer to achieve their outcome
- Your product covers: ____%
- Partner products needed: ____
- Professional services needed: ____
- Integrations required: ____

**3b. Build or partner for gaps**
- You don't need to build everything — you need to ensure it exists and is easy to acquire
- Create a preferred partner list covering the gaps
- Document the "out-of-the-box" solution in a solution brief that addresses the complete use case

**3c. Write the Whole Product Scenario**
A one-page document describing the complete experience:
- Day 1: What does the customer install/integrate?
- Week 1: What does success look like?
- Month 3: What is the measurable business outcome?

This scenario becomes your sales narrative and partner recruitment tool.

## Step 4: Position Against Competitors (Not Against "Status Quo")

In the mainstream market, positioning works differently. Pragmatists evaluate alternatives; they do not buy something with no alternatives.

**4a. Name the competition explicitly**
- Do not say "there's nothing like us" — pragmatists distrust this
- Position against the market leader as: "[Your product] is [market leader] but [your differentiator] for [specific segment]"
- Example: "Notion is Confluence but designed for small teams who need flexible structure without IT overhead"

**4b. Create a competitive comparison table**
- List the 3-4 most common alternatives the beachhead segment considers
- Highlight the 2-3 features that matter most to pragmatists in that segment
- Show where you win decisively on those criteria

**4c. Own the market category**
- Pragmatists buy from market leaders — if you can't be #1 in a large category, redefine the category
- Name a new category where you are the obvious leader
- Example: Salesforce did not win "CRM market" initially — they won "hosted CRM" then "cloud CRM" then renamed the category

## Step 5: Build Reference Customers

Pragmatists buy from other pragmatists. You need reference customers who look like the beachhead segment.

**5a. Identify your target reference profile**
- 3-5 customers in the beachhead segment who are respected by their peers
- They must be willing to take calls from prospects and have achieved measurable results

**5b. Structure reference-customer deals**
- Offer significant pricing, extra support, or co-marketing in exchange for reference commitment
- Document the agreement: they will take reference calls, participate in case studies, and speak at events
- Set a results timeline: by month 3, they should have a quotable outcome

**5c. Create the reference evidence package**
- Written case study with before/after metrics
- Video testimonial (2-3 minutes)
- Logo permission for website and sales materials
- Reference call availability (limit to 1-2 calls/month per reference)

## Step 6: Target Distribution Channels

The mainstream market requires different channels than early adopters.

**6a. Map pragmatist information sources**
- Where does the beachhead segment get recommendations? (Analyst reports, peer communities, industry associations)
- Which influencers do they trust? (Not social media influencers — industry analysts, consultants, conference speakers)

**6b. Invest in analyst relations**
- If Gartner or Forrester covers your category, engage them — pragmatists buy from Gartner Magic Quadrant leaders
- If no analyst covers your category, create the category definition and pitch it to analysts

**6c. Build the partner channel**
- Mainstream customers buy through trusted advisors (VARs, consultants, system integrators)
- Identify 2-3 partners who already serve your beachhead segment and integrate your product into their service offering

## Common Pitfalls

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Targeting too broad a segment | No word-of-mouth, no reference customers | Pick one specific, named segment |
| Skipping whole product work | Pragmatists can't adopt → sales stall | Map and fill whole product gaps before launch |
| Positioning against "no competition" | Pragmatists don't trust category-of-one claims | Name specific alternatives, show comparative advantage |
| Using early adopter references | Pragmatists distrust visionary references | Build reference customers who are pragmatists |
| Moving to next segment too early | Beachhead not fully won → no bowling pin effect | Stay until you have 50%+ beachhead segment market share |

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Adoption lifecycle stage diagnosed with evidence
- [ ] 3-5 beachhead candidates scored on 6 criteria
- [ ] Beachhead segment selected and documented with named buyer profile
- [ ] Whole product gap map completed
- [ ] Competitive positioning statement written (vs. named alternatives)
- [ ] 3 target reference customers identified with deal structure
- [ ] Bowling pin expansion map drawn (beachhead → 3 adjacent segments)
- [ ] Primary distribution channel for pragmatists identified
