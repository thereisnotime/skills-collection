# Made to Stick - Implementation Guide

Step-by-step methodology for creating messages that are understood, remembered, and drive action using the SUCCESs framework from Chip and Dan Heath.

## Step 1: Find the Core Idea

Before applying any SUCCESs principles, identify your single most important idea. Everything else supports this core.

**1a. The Commander's Intent technique**
- Military commanders state one clear, simple goal before a mission so everyone can improvise toward it
- Write your core idea in one sentence, without jargon: "If the audience remembers nothing else, they remember ___"
- If you can't say it in one sentence, it's not simple enough yet

**1b. The inverted pyramid**
- Start with the most critical information, then add detail
- Journalists never bury the lead. Most communicators always do.
- Test: if someone reads only your first sentence, do they get the core idea?

**1c. The "but and therefore" test**
- A series of facts connected by "and then" is not a core idea — it's a list
- A core idea has a tension: "The product is designed to save time, BUT people actually spend more time with it because they love it so much"
- "But and therefore" creates narrative pull; "and then" is just a sequence

## Step 2: Apply the SUCCESs Checklist

Rate your current message or content against each principle (0-2):

| Principle | Checklist Question | Score |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Simple | Can your core idea be stated in one memorable sentence? | /2 |
| Unexpected | Does it violate a common assumption or expectation? | /2 |
| Concrete | Does it use sensory details, specific examples, or tangible imagery? | /2 |
| Credible | Does it have vivid statistics, authorities, or anti-authority credibility? | /2 |
| Emotional | Does it make people feel something? Does it connect to self-interest or identity? | /2 |
| Stories | Is it wrapped in a narrative that creates simulation and inspiration? | /2 |

Score < 8 = message will be forgotten. Rewrite the weakest principles first.

## Step 3: Make It Unexpected

The brain disengages from expected information. Unexpected information breaks the pattern and commands attention.

**3a. Identify the common assumption to break**
- What does your audience assume about this topic?
- State the assumption, then break it dramatically
- Example: "Most people think more choice is always better. It's not. More choice makes people less happy and less likely to buy."

**3b. Create a curiosity gap**
- Tease what's coming without revealing it: "Here's the thing almost every company gets wrong about customer retention"
- Questions are more sticky than statements: "What do Nobel Prize winners and kindergarteners have in common?"
- Mystery: present an open question that the audience wants to resolve, then build the answer

**3c. The Velcro Theory of memory**
- Information sticks to existing mental schemas — but unexpected information creates a NEW schema that's even more memorable
- Structure: set up the familiar pattern, then break it at the pivotal moment

## Step 4: Make It Concrete

Abstract ideas are forgotten. Concrete, sensory details are remembered.

**4a. Replace abstractions with specific imagery**
- Abstract: "Our software improves team efficiency."
- Concrete: "Teams that use our software ship features 3x faster. That's a feature that used to take 6 weeks done in 2."

**4b. Use the "CNN Test"**
- Would a CNN reporter be able to film this?
- Abstract concepts can't be filmed. Concrete ones can.
- "Strategic alignment" can't be filmed. "The engineering team and the product team met every Monday to review the same prioritized list" can.

**4c. Use analogies to make the abstract concrete**
- Map an unfamiliar, abstract concept onto a familiar, concrete one
- "Our API is like a power outlet — you plug in your app and it just works, without knowing how the electricity flows"

## Step 5: Build Credibility

People need a reason to believe. Credibility sources vary depending on audience skepticism.

**5a. Use statistics, not trends**
- "9 out of 10 dentists" is sticky. "Dentists prefer..." is not.
- Make numbers tangible: "The human body contains enough fat to make 7 bars of soap"
- Use scales that relate to human experience: not "40,000 miles of blood vessels" but "enough blood vessels to wrap around the Earth 1.6 times"

**5b. Anti-authority credibility**
- Sometimes the most credible voice is a skeptic who was converted, not an expert who always believed
- Testimonials that start with "I was skeptical at first..." are more credible than unconditional praise
- Real user examples with specific numbers outperform celebrity endorsements for skeptical audiences

**5c. The "Sinatra Test"**
- "If I can make it here, I can make it anywhere"
- One extreme, undeniable example that proves the rule for all other cases
- Example: "If this security system protects nuclear power plants, it can handle your payroll data"

## Step 6: Create Emotional Connection

People don't act on information; they act on feelings. Emotion bridges the gap between knowing and doing.

**6a. Appeal to self-interest (not in the selfish sense)**
- Answer: "What's in it for me?" — specifically and personally
- Not "Our product saves money for companies" but "What would you do with 10 extra hours every month?"

**6b. Appeal to identity**
- People do things because of who they are, or who they want to be
- "Don't be the kind of person who..." or "People like you..."
- Example: "Don't Mess with Texas" worked because it appealed to Texan identity, not just anti-litter facts

**6c. Activate the WIIFY ("What's In It For You")**
- Every message must be filtered through: does the audience personally care about this?
- Test: could someone respond "So what?" to your message without missing the point?

## Step 7: Embed in a Story

Stories are the oldest and most powerful knowledge-transfer technology. They work because they create mental simulation (we live through the story) and inspiration (we see what's possible).

**7a. Three story archetypes (highest impact):**

| Archetype | Pattern | Use Case |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Challenge | Protagonist overcomes a formidable obstacle | Motivating action, overcoming fear of trying |
| Connection | Bridges gap between people; builds relationships | Building empathy, community |
| Creativity | Finding a nonobvious solution to a difficult problem | Inspiring innovation, lateral thinking |

**7b. Springboard stories**
- A springboard story makes change feel possible and concrete, without laying out a detailed plan
- It shows one specific case, then implies the broader possibility
- Example: IBM changed its entire approach after one manager told a story of one sale that used a new approach

**7c. Story structure (minimum viable version)**
1. Character in a specific situation
2. An obstacle or challenge
3. A resolution that illustrates your core message
- Do not skip the obstacle — tension is what makes stories sticky

## Common Pitfalls

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| The Curse of Knowledge | Expert assumes audience knows what expert knows | Use concrete examples and analogies |
| Too many core messages | Audience remembers nothing | One core idea only |
| Leading with data | Audience zones out | Lead with the story; use data as evidence |
| Abstract benefits | Not memorable or actionable | Replace every abstract claim with a concrete image |
| Informational content | Not emotionally sticky | Every message needs an emotional hook |

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Core idea stated in one sentence (no jargon)
- [ ] SUCCESs audit scored per principle
- [ ] One assumption identified and broken for "Unexpected"
- [ ] Every abstract claim replaced with a concrete image or specific number
- [ ] Credibility built with tangible statistics or anti-authority testimonials
- [ ] Emotional hook identified: self-interest or identity appeal
- [ ] At least one story identified that illustrates the core message
- [ ] "CNN Test" passed: every key concept is imageable
