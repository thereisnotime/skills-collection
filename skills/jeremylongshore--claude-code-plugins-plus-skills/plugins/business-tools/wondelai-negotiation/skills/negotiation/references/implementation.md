# Negotiation - Implementation Guide

Step-by-step methodology for preparing and executing negotiations using Chris Voss's tactical empathy approach from "Never Split the Difference."

## Step 1: Preparation Framework

Before any negotiation, complete this preparation document:

**1a. Black Swan Discovery**
- Black Swans = unknown unknowns that, if revealed, would change everything
- Ask yourself: "What could the other side know that I don't, that would make their position completely rational?"
- Think about: their constraints, deadlines, internal politics, alternatives they haven't disclosed
- Your goal: surface these during the negotiation through listening, not interrogation

**1b. Accusation Audit**
- List every negative thought the other side might have about you, your company, or your proposal
- Be thorough — even accusations that feel unfair
- Example for salary negotiation: "They'll think I'm being greedy," "They'll wonder if I'll leave if they say no," "They'll question whether I'm worth it"
- You will address these upfront before they become objections

**1c. Define your ranges**
- Target: your realistic goal
- Stretch: your highest/lowest defensible anchor
- Walk-away (BATNA): your Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — the point at which you walk away
- Never enter without knowing your walk-away. A bad deal is worse than no deal.

## Step 2: Open with Tactical Empathy

Tactical empathy = understanding the emotions and perspective of the other party AND making them feel understood. This is not agreement — it is acknowledgment.

**2a. Start the Accusation Audit upfront**
- Defuse negative emotions by naming them before the other side raises them
- Opens with: "I know this might feel like I'm not valuing the relationship by pushing on this..." or "I realize this timing might be challenging for your team..."
- When you name it first, it loses its power as an objection

**2b. Use labels (not assumptions)**
- Labels reflect the emotion back without judgment or interpretation
- Format: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It feels like..."
- NOT: "I know you're frustrated" (interpretive assumption)
- YES: "It seems like there might be some concerns about the timeline" (opens space for them to clarify)
- After a label: go silent. Let them fill the silence.

**2c. Use mirrors**
- Mirror = repeat the last 1-3 words of what they said, with upward inflection
- Creates connection without revealing your position
- Example: They say "We have budget constraints this quarter." You say: "Budget constraints?" They continue explaining — you learn their real situation.

## Step 3: Discover Their World with Calibrated Questions

Calibrated questions are open-ended questions that begin with "How" or "What." They give the other side the illusion of control while you gather information and guide the conversation.

**3a. Core calibrated questions**

| Situation | Calibrated Question |
|-----------|-------------------|
| They say no | "How am I supposed to do that?" |
| They make an impossible ask | "What makes this the right number?" |
| They stall | "What's making this difficult?" |
| Hidden decision-maker | "How does this decision get made on your end?" |
| Need to understand their goals | "What does success look like for you here?" |
| Testing their commitment | "How will this impact everything else you're working on?" |

**3b. Never ask "Why"**
- "Why" sounds accusatory and puts people on the defensive
- Replace every "Why" with "What" or "How": "Why did you price it that way?" → "What went into that pricing decision?"

**3c. Get to "That's right"**
- Your goal is to get them to say "That's right" (not "You're right")
- "That's right" = genuine agreement where they feel understood
- "You're right" = they want to end the conversation, not genuine alignment
- Sequence: summarize their position fully and accurately → they say "That's right" → now you can move

## Step 4: Shape Their Reality (Anchoring and Bending Reality)

**4a. Extreme anchor**
- The first number in any negotiation has disproportionate influence
- Anchor first with an extreme-but-defensible number
- For salary: anchor high and attach a "ridiculous" reason — the reason reduces resistance
- Example: "I'm looking for $185,000. I know that might sound high, but I've done the math on what I can contribute to pipeline growth based on my track record."

**4b. Use the Ackerman model for price negotiations**
- A structured bargaining sequence that makes the other side feel they got a good deal:
  1. Open at your extreme anchor (e.g., 1.65x your target)
  2. Move to 1.25x target after first counter
  3. Move to 1.10x target after second counter
  4. Move to 1.03x target after third counter
  5. Move to your target (1.00x) — with precision and emotion: "This is truly my best offer"
- Each concession is smaller than the last → signals you're approaching your real limit
- Add a non-monetary item (not worth much to you) at the last stage → makes the final number feel like a true limit

**4c. Leverage deadlines and loss aversion**
- People are more motivated by avoiding loss than achieving gain
- Reframe gains as loss prevention: not "you could gain $50k in revenue" but "right now you're losing $50k/month by not having this"
- Deadlines create urgency — but reveal your deadline only when it benefits you

## Step 5: Handle the "No" and Difficult Emotions

**5a. "No" is not the end — it's the beginning**
- "No" gives the other side a sense of safety and control
- After a "No": "I completely understand. Can I ask — what would need to be true for this to work?"
- Or the calibrated question: "What would make this the wrong decision?"

**5b. Use the Late-Night FM DJ voice for sensitive moments**
- When delivering difficult news or navigating emotional tension: slow down, lower your voice, use a calm, warm tone
- This voice de-escalates emotional conversations before they spiral

**5c. The Accusation Audit for live objections**
- When they raise an objection, don't immediately counter it — label it first
- "It seems like the price is a significant concern" (pause) → let them confirm or clarify
- Only after they feel heard should you respond to the substance

## Step 6: Close with "How" Questions

Never close by asking "Do we have a deal?" — that creates pressure and invites "no."

**6a. Implementation questions force commitment**
- "How do we make this happen on your end?"
- "How do we move forward from here?"
- "What would you need from us to get started?"
- These questions presuppose agreement and require the other side to describe their path forward

**6b. Get three "Yes"es**
- A single "Yes" at the end of a negotiation is fragile
- Get three genuine confirmations throughout: on the problem, on the solution, on the terms
- "Yes" to the problem: "It sounds like this timeline pressure is real?" — "Yes."
- "Yes" to the solution: "It sounds like our timeline would actually solve that?" — "Yes."
- "Yes" to the terms: "So if we can get this moving by Friday, that works?" — "Yes."

## Common Pitfalls

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|------------|-----|
| Splitting the difference | Both sides lose; bad deal for at least one | No deal is better than a bad deal |
| Arguing on the merits | Logical arguments trigger resistance | Emotional validation first, logic second |
| Revealing BATNA too early | Weakens your position | Never disclose your walk-away unless absolutely necessary |
| Accepting first offer | Signals you didn't anchor high enough | Always counter, even if the first offer is good |
| Forgetting Black Swans | Missing the real lever in the negotiation | Stay curious; something you don't know is affecting their behavior |

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Black Swan list: 3 unknown unknowns that could change everything
- [ ] Accusation Audit: 5+ negative assumptions the other side might have
- [ ] Ranges defined: target, stretch anchor, BATNA
- [ ] Calibrated questions prepared for each likely obstacle
- [ ] Ackerman sequence calculated if this is a price negotiation
- [ ] Opening label/mirror prepared to start with tactical empathy
- [ ] "That's right" moment planned: how will you summarize their position?
- [ ] Three implementation questions ready to close
