# Element Examples

Good and bad examples for each core element. Use to calibrate quality and push
for specificity.

---

## Reader

### Weak Examples

**Too broad:**

> "Entrepreneurs who want to grow their business"

_Problem: This is a category, not a person. Can't make decisions about what to
include/exclude._

**Demographics only:**

> "Women aged 25-40 who work in tech"

_Problem: Demographics don't reveal problems, struggles, or what they've tried._

**Wishful thinking:**

> "Anyone who wants to be more productive"

_Problem: "Anyone" means no one. No specific situation or pain point._

### Strong Examples

**Specific situation:**

> "First-time founders in their first 18 months who are drowning in advice.
> They've read the startup books, listened to the podcasts, but have too many
> frameworks and not enough clarity on what to actually do Monday morning.
> They're overwhelmed, not uninformed."

_Why it works: Specific stage, specific problem (overwhelm from too much, not
too little), implies what they've tried._

**Clear pain point:**

> "Mid-career professionals who've been promoted into management but never
> learned how to give feedback. They avoid hard conversations, their team's
> performance suffers, and they're starting to wonder if they're cut out for
> leadership."

_Why it works: Specific transition point, specific skill gap, emotional stakes
clear._

**Identifiable person:**

> "The person who bought three productivity apps last month, set them up with
> enthusiasm, and abandoned all of them within two weeks. They don't need
> another system — they need to understand why systems keep failing them."

_Why it works: You can picture this person. You probably know them. The problem
is specific and behavioral._

## Transformation

### Weak Examples

**Vague improvement:**

> "Before: Unproductive. After: Productive."

_Problem: These aren't states you can picture. No concrete difference._

**Too ambitious:**

> "Before: Struggling writer. After: Bestselling author."

_Problem: An ebook can't deliver this. Overpromises._

**Just information transfer:**

> "Before: Doesn't know about X. After: Knows about X."

_Problem: Knowledge isn't transformation. What can they DO differently?_

### Strong Examples

**Concrete behavior change:**

> "Before: Stares at blank page, writes in painful bursts, rarely finishes
> anything.  
> After: Sits down with a clear process, writes consistently for 30 minutes,
> completes drafts."

_Why it works: You can observe the before and after. Specific behaviors, not
vague states._

**Mindset shift with evidence:**

> "Before: Believes they're 'not a math person' and avoids anything
> quantitative.  
> After: Comfortable with basic data analysis, actually enjoys finding insights
> in numbers, no longer opts out of quantitative conversations."

_Why it works: Internal shift (belief) paired with external evidence (behavior
change)._

**Specific capability:**

> "Before: Has ideas for YouTube videos but no idea how to structure them —
> rambles, loses viewers.  
> After: Can outline a video in 15 minutes using a proven structure that hooks
> and holds attention."

_Why it works: Specific skill, specific time frame, clear before/after
observable states._

## Promise

### Weak Examples

**Vague benefit:**

> "Learn to be more effective"

_Problem: "More effective" at what? Says nothing specific._

**Feature-focused:**

> "A complete guide to email marketing"

_Problem: Describes what the ebook IS, not what the reader GETS._

**Overpromising:**

> "Transform your entire life in one weekend"

_Problem: Unbelievable. Triggers skepticism._

### Strong Examples

**Specific outcome:**

> "Write your first Zettelkasten note today and never lose a good idea again."

_Why it works: Specific action ("write your first note"), specific timeline
("today"), specific benefit ("never lose a good idea")._

**Problem → solution:**

> "Stop drowning in bookmarks you'll never revisit — build a personal knowledge
> system that actually resurfaces what you need, when you need it."

_Why it works: Names the pain precisely, promises a specific solution._

**Transformation in a sentence:**

> "Go from 'I should post more' to a sustainable content system that runs on 3
> hours a week."

_Why it works: Captures the before/after in one line. Specific and believable
("3 hours a week")._

## Content Source

### Weak Examples

**Vague reference:**

> "Based on my experience"

_Problem: What experience? How validated? What specifically exists?_

**Assumed from nothing:**

> "I'll write it all from scratch based on what I know"

_Problem: May work, but no validation that the content resonates. Higher risk._

### Strong Examples

**Validated content:**

> "Based on my 12-part YouTube series on Zettelkasten (47K total views). The
> 'numbering system' video (8K views) generated 200+ comments asking for more
> detail — that's the core of this ebook."

_Why it works: Specific content identified, audience validation (views,
comments), clear signal about what resonates._

**Content inventory:**

> "I have: (1) A newsletter series on feedback conversations (6 issues, 45% avg
> open rate), (2) A workshop I've run 4 times with 50+ participants, (3) The
> slide deck and exercises from that workshop. Gaps: Need to write the
> 'difficult personalities' chapter from scratch."

_Why it works: Specific inventory, validation signals (open rate, repeat
workshop), gaps identified._

**Book extraction:**

> "Extracting the 'Morning Routine' section from my larger book project. It's 3
> chapters (roughly 8,000 words already drafted) that stand alone. The full book
> won't be ready for 8 months, but this section is complete enough to validate
> interest."

_Why it works: Clear source, content already exists, strategic rationale for
extraction._

## Scope & Format

### Weak Examples

**Undefined:**

> "However long it needs to be"

_Problem: No constraint means no discipline. Will likely bloat or meander._

**Format without rationale:**

> "It'll be a workbook"

_Problem: Why a workbook? Does the content actually fit that format?_

**Everything included:**

> "Covers the complete history, theory, and practice of X"

_Problem: This is a book, not an ebook. Scope too broad._

### Strong Examples

**Tight scope:**

> "12,000-15,000 words. 6 chapters focused ONLY on the daily capture process —
> not organization, not retrieval, not the full system. Someone finishing this
> will have a working capture habit, nothing more."

_Why it works: Word count range, chapter count, explicit boundary ("ONLY"),
clear outcome._

**Format with rationale:**

> "Prose chapters with one 'Try This' exercise per chapter. Not a full workbook
> because the transformation is mostly understanding, with light application.
> Exercises are for reinforcement, not the main event."

_Why it works: Format chosen deliberately, rationale for what it's NOT._

**Platform-aware:**

> "KDP primary (for discoverability), priced at $4.99. Short enough to read in
> one sitting (under 2 hours). No companion files needed — everything works
> within the ebook itself."

_Why it works: Platform choice explained, reading time considered, delivery
constraints clear._

## Value Gap (Situational)

### Weak Examples

**Just reorganization:**

> "It's my YouTube content in written form"

_Problem: Why would someone pay for content they can watch free? Format change
isn't enough._

**Vague addition:**

> "Goes deeper than my free content"

_Problem: Deeper how? This needs to be specific._

### Strong Examples

**Clear additional value:**

> "My YouTube videos show WHAT to do. This ebook explains WHY it works and HOW
> to troubleshoot when it doesn't. Viewers who've tried and struggled need the
> context I didn't have time to include in video format."

_Why it works: Specific gap (what vs. why/how), identifies who needs it (viewers
who've tried and struggled)._

**Reference value:**

> "The video is 45 minutes of teaching. The ebook is the permanent reference —
> checklists, templates, and quick-lookup guides you can use while implementing.
> Watch once, reference forever."

_Why it works: Different use case (learning vs. reference), clear ongoing
value._

## Enemy (Situational)

### Weak Examples

**Vague opponent:**

> "Bad advice out there"

_Problem: Too vague. What bad advice? From whom?_

**Strawman:**

> "People who don't care about quality"

_Problem: No one self-identifies this way. Not a real enemy._

### Strong Examples

**Specific conventional wisdom:**

> "The enemy is 'write every day' advice. It sounds virtuous but creates guilt
> spirals for people with unpredictable schedules. This ebook argues for 'write
> consistently' instead — which might mean 3x/week, and that's fine."

_Why it works: Names specific advice, explains why it's harmful, offers clear
alternative._

**Named mindset:**

> "The enemy is 'productivity porn' — the endless consumption of systems, tools,
> and tips that feels productive but produces nothing. This ebook is the
> antidote: one simple system, no optimization rabbit holes."

_Why it works: Named and recognizable pattern, reader can identify if this is
them, clear positioning against it._

---

## Using These Examples

During concept development:

1. **Compare drafts to examples** — Is the Reader closer to weak or strong?
2. **Push for specificity** — "Can we make this more like [strong example]?"
3. **Name the problem** — "This is feature-focused, not benefit-focused. What
   does the reader GET?"
4. **Calibrate ambition** — "Is this transformation achievable in ebook scope?"
